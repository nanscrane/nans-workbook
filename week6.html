Data in your pocket

Unlike our group's classification, I classified my own items based on functionality. They are divided into paper and pens needed for my studies and the survey reports I need to write; wet wipes, dental floss, and a water bottle needed for my daily life; a wallet for traveling and an iPad for entertainment. From the different classifications of items by each person, we can see that categorizing data is actually a very personalized matter. The methods each person uses to classify data are mostly based on individual personalities.

During the class, we conducted an activity: we collected items from our backpacks and others' backpacks, and after discussing them, we classified these items. Our group's classification was based on 'the degree to which you most want others to see and least want others to see the items.' The items on the far left of the picture are the ones you most want others to see, while the items on the far right are the ones you least want others to see.

Data visualization

Another example is the SoundVisualizer by Ashley Thompson. The author uses lines and circles to express changes in sound. By making the circles larger or smaller, it more intuitively conveys to the audience the increase or decrease in volume. When people can perceive an object from both visual and auditory perspectives, it provides a more comprehensive feeling about that object.

After categorizing our own data, we need to think about how to transform these mundane numbers into more intuitive elements such as graphics, lines, and colors. The data visualization 'pie' that we learned this week is a great example of visualizing data. Using different colors to represent different types of data is one of the most intuitive ways.
